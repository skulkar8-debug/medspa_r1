04 / 12   Regional Analysis
Target Scale Does Not Translate to Market Efficiency
Plotting supply density against STOC scores by region reveals that high-density markets are frequently over-served while lower-density markets carry suppressed but recoverable demand. The opportunity signal is inversely related to headline market size.
South
High STOC + lower BD density. Latent demand outpaces current supply. Strongest net opportunity region for both acquisition and de novo entry.
Northeast
High supply density + lower relative STOC. Saturation compresses net opportunity. Late-entry dynamics predominate in most top metros.
Midwest
Balanced positioning across both axes. Consistent, scalable market architecture. Most reliable mid-density rollup conditions nationally.
West
Wide dispersion across both metrics. Inconsistent opportunity. Requires CBSA-level filtering before capital is committed.
Analytical Finding
Opportunity is driven by supply-demand imbalance, not absolute scale. High total target count does not imply high-quality entry conditions. Geography is a proxy, not a strategy. The STOC score normalizes for this bias.

05 / 12   Market Typology
Mid-Density Markets Offer the Highest Risk-Adjusted Opportunity
Segmenting markets by target density type reveals that dense urban environments face significant supply pressure, while mid-density growth corridors carry the highest composite STOC score with minimal PE competition. Whitespace is not rural.
Dense Urban
High supply saturation. Low gap. Elevated PE presence. Entry window narrowing materially in most top-10 metros nationally.
Mid-Density
Highest STOC composite. High supply gap. Minimal PE competition. Priority tier for acquisition and de novo deployment.
Suburban Growth
Low supply, variable gap. Demand emerging. De novo-ready in select markets with strong demographic and income tailwinds.

Low supply, low STOC. Demand insufficient at current growth rates. Selective entry only on a case-by-case basis.
Segmentation Finding
Whitespace is concentrated in mid-density growth markets with limited scaled competition and recoverable demand curves. This segmentation removes geography-first bias and replaces it with structure-first decisioning logic.

06 / 12   Target Distribution
Independent Targets Cluster Outside Major Metro Markets
Acquisition-ready independent targets do not concentrate in top-10 metros. The highest target density by CBSA skews toward mid-size, high-growth markets where consolidation has not yet begun and independent owners have not yet faced PE-driven competitive pricing pressure.
Structural Observation
Acquisition opportunity is driven by independent target density, not total market size. Top supply cities are not top acquisition cities. Capital allocated purely to gateway metros undershoots total addressable opportunity by a material margin.
Deployment Implication
The highest-density independent targets are in Tier 2 and Tier 3 CBSAs. These markets are also the least competitively pressured, offering the best combination of target volume and pricing leverage on acquisition multiples.

09 / 12   Competitive Pressure
Competitive Pressure Reduces Opportunity Non-Linearly
PE neighbor count is not a linear discount to market quality. Once PE penetration crosses threshold levels in a CBSA, opportunity compression accelerates sharply. Entry 12 to 24 months post-PE arrival carries materially higher cost and lower upside.
Prime Entry
Low PE presence, high STOC score. Maximum opportunity window. Priority deployment with full strategic optionality intact.
Late Entry
High PE, high STOC. Quality market with compressed timeline and elevated acquisition pricing. Selective entry only.
Avoid

Monitor
Low PE, low STOC. Insufficient demand signal. Watch list only until structural conditions improve.
Timing Thesis
Timing matters more than demand level. PE presence compresses opportunity windows even in high-demand markets. The STOC system tracks PE arrival in real time to identify the inflection point before it becomes a pricing event on acquisition targets.

10 / 12   Consolidation Structure
Consolidation Is Early But Clustering Rapidly
PE penetration across CBSAs is not uniform or gradual. The distribution is highly right-skewed: the majority of markets remain at or near zero, but a small cluster of markets are consolidating at speed. First-mover entry captures the largest multiple expansion opportunity.
Structural Observation
Consolidation does not happen gradually across the market. It occurs in localized bursts in specific CBSAs driven by first-mover platform entry. Once it starts, it compounds quickly and acquisition pricing follows within 18 to 36 months.
